The thread that runs through everything

Across every guide, every hack, every expert interview, three ideas keep showing up. They are not technical. They are the things that separate people who got real value out of AI from people who stayed busy.

1. Capture what works.

When you nail a result, you do not have a result. You have a process. Save the process. Turn the chat into a playbook. Turn the playbook into a Custom GPT, a Gem, or a Claude Skill. Stop rebuilding from scratch every Monday.

2. Lead with the problem, not the tool.

Tools change every week. Problems do not. Define the recurring task you want to win. Then choose the tool. Sabrina Ramonov calls this a "problem-first AI system." It is the only thing that scales.

3. Structure beats cleverness.

A clear five-block prompt — instruction, context, format, tone, clarifying questions — beats a long, wandering one every time. Frameworks are not handcuffs. They are how you stop fixing answers and start using them.

If you only do five things from this whole course

  1. Adopt the 5-block prompt template. Save it. Use it once today. → Cheat sheet
  2. Pick one chat where you nailed it. Turn it into a playbook. → The 3-step method
  3. Set up one Custom GPT, Gem, or Claude Skill for a task you do every week.
  4. Build a Reply Library for the messages you keep rewriting.
  5. Run one weekly cadence: Monday brain-dump, mid-week new tool, Friday review.

Day 01

Get fluent. Stop fearing the blank chat box.

You learn what AI actually is, how the four big tools differ, and the single most important skill: writing a clear prompt. By the end of today, you can sit down at a blank chat box and not flinch.

Lesson 1.1 — Getting Started With ChatGPT (Even If You've Tried It Before)

Most people who say "I've tried ChatGPT" tried the free version, asked it three things, didn't get magic, and walked away. That's a fair reaction to a bad first date. The actual product, used well, is something else.

The first move is to ignore the model picker. The defaults are fine. Pick one model, stop tab-switching. The second move is to stop asking AI to "help with" things. Ask for a deliverable. "Help me with my LinkedIn post" yields advice. "Write a 150-word LinkedIn post about X for an audience of Y, in my voice (here's a sample), then suggest two alternate hooks" yields a draft.

The third move is to stay in the same chat for a single task. Don't open a new conversation every time. The chat is your context. When you walk away from it, you walk away from everything you taught it.

Lesson 1.2 — The 5-Block Prompt Framework

This is the foundational skill. Every prompt has up to five blocks: Instruction (what to do), Context (background it needs), Format (how the answer should look), Tone (how it should sound), and Clarifying Questions (permission to ask before guessing). Use as many as the task needs. For a translation, just an instruction. For a client email, all five.

The block that does the most work for the least effort is the last one. Adding "If you need more information or clarification to do this well and help me reach my goal, ask me the questions you need before you answer" stops AI from guessing — which is where most bad answers come from.

The reusable template
Instruction: [What you want the AI to do.]
Context: [Relevant background information.]
Format: [How the output should be structured.]
Tone: [Communication style if relevant.]
Clarifying Questions: If you need more information or clarification to do this well and help me reach my goal, ask me the questions you need before you answer.

Why this matters: a vague prompt gets a vague answer, then you spend ten minutes correcting it. A clear prompt gets a usable answer the first time. The five blocks force you to be specific about what you want before you ask for it. The thinking is the work.

Lesson 1.3 — ChatGPT CheatSheet: All Features and Models Explained

The interface has more in it than people realize. Memory stores what AI should remember about you across all chats (your role, your tone, your projects). Custom Instructions are your standing prompt — set once, applied to everything. Projects are scoped workspaces with their own memory and files. Canvas is collaborative editing for long output. Tasks let you schedule prompts to run on a schedule.

Models: pick a default and stick with it. The reasoning models (o-series) are slower but better at multi-step problems. The fast models are better for chat. Switch only when you hit a wall, not as a habit.

Lesson 1.4 — Keep Your Data Private

For both ChatGPT and Claude: turn off "training on my data" in settings. Don't paste anything you wouldn't paste into a Google Doc you might accidentally make public. Treat every chat like it could be read someday. Most of what you'll do is fine. Some things — client data, anything under NDA, anything regulated — needs the workspace/team plan or shouldn't go in at all.

The honest answer is that the privacy story is good now but evolving. Default to the conservative read.

By end of day one: you can write a prompt that gets you 80% of the way without rewrites. You know which model to use and when. Your data settings are sane.

Day 02

Set up your AI workspace.

You stop typing the same instructions every time. You build the foundations that make every future chat faster. Today is the difference between using AI and operating on AI.

Lesson 2.1 — Understanding ChatGPT Projects

A Project is not a folder. It's a scoped workspace with its own memory, its own files, and its own custom instructions. Every chat inside a Project inherits all of that. So if you set up a "Client X" Project with their brand voice document, their style guide, and instructions to write in their tone — every chat you start in that Project knows it without being told.

The right setup: one Project per ongoing context. A Project for your business. A Project for each major client. A Project called "Personal" for your life. Drop reference docs in. Add custom instructions that explain the role AI is playing in that Project. Now the work happens fast.

Lesson 2.2 — Custom GPTs: Build Your First One

A Custom GPT is a prompt-plus-files-plus-personality wrapped into a button. You build it once, you click it forever. Where Projects are scoped contexts you live in, GPTs are tools you call. "LinkedIn Post Writer" is a GPT. "Receipt Categorizer" is a GPT. "Brutal First-Draft Editor" is a GPT.

The setup is three fields: Instructions (the system prompt — what role does this GPT play, what format, what to avoid), Knowledge (files it should always reference), and Conversation Starters (the prompts you click to begin). Build a GPT for any task you do more than three times.

Custom GPT instructions template
You are [role]. You help me [outcome] by [method].

Always:
- [non-negotiable 1]
- [non-negotiable 2]

Never:
- [thing to avoid]

Format your output as: [structure]
Tone: [tone]

If you need clarification before producing output, ask first.

Lesson 2.3 — Gemini Gems

Gems are Google's version of Custom GPTs. Same idea, different ecosystem. The reason to care: Gemini is wired into Google Workspace. A Gem can pull from your Drive, Gmail, Calendar without you uploading anything. If you live in Workspace, that integration is the unlock.

Build the same Gem you'd build as a Custom GPT, then point it at your Drive folders. The "Reusable Newsletter Writer" Gem that has access to your Drive folder of past newsletters is qualitatively better than the GPT version.

Lesson 2.4 — Claude Skills

The newest piece of the stack. A Skill is a folder of instructions and reference files that Claude loads when relevant. Where a GPT is a button you click, a Skill is a capability Claude reaches for automatically when the task fits.

The classic Skill: your voice. Drop in samples of your writing, instructions on tone, things to avoid. Now any time you ask Claude to write, it writes like you — without you having to remind it. Skills are also the right place to encode style guides, format conventions, brand rules, anything that should be on by default.

Lesson 2.5 — The Personalization Stack

You now have four layers of personalization in ChatGPT alone: Memory (always-on, lightweight, stores facts about you), Custom Instructions (always-on, sets defaults), Projects (scoped, workspace-level), Custom GPTs (called explicitly, task-level). The mistake is dumping everything into Memory. The fix is layering: high-frequency facts in Memory, defaults in Custom Instructions, contexts in Projects, tools as GPTs.

By end of day two: you have at least one Custom GPT, one Gem or Skill, and three Projects running. Setup happens once. Output happens fast.

Day 03

Make AI write in your voice.

Generic AI sounds like everyone else. That's why people clock AI-written content immediately — sentences too round, vocabulary too even, no edges. The fix is teaching it your edges once, not every time.

Lesson 3.1 — Format Information in Markdown

This sounds technical. It isn't. Markdown is just a way of typing structure: # Heading, - bullet, **bold**. AI parses Markdown dramatically better than prose. When you give it a long brief, format the brief in Markdown. Headers for sections. Bullets for lists. Bold for emphasis. AI reads the structure as structure, not as words to wade through.

The same applies in reverse: ask for output in Markdown. It's easier to scan, easier to paste into Notion or Docs, easier to reformat downstream.

Lesson 3.2 — Move Between Tools Without Losing Format

The trick: ask for the response in Markdown, then paste it into a tool that renders Markdown (Notion, Obsidian, most modern editors). For Word and Google Docs, ask AI to "format this as the actual structured document" — it'll use real headings and lists instead of **bold** literals. For email, ask for "plain text, no Markdown formatting."

Lesson 3.3 — Dean's Top 10 (Insanely Simple) Prompts He Uses Every Week

The founder's actual workflow. The pattern across all ten is mundane: most weekly value comes from a small set of repeatable prompts. The categories: summarize (turn a long thing into a short thing), extract (pull specific items out of a longer thing), restructure (same content, different format), suggest (alternatives, options, next steps), critique (find what's weak before someone else does).

The five workhorses
1. "Summarize this in [N] bullets, focused on [angle]."
2. "Extract every [item type] mentioned and put them in a table with [columns]."
3. "Rewrite this as [format] for [audience]."
4. "Give me three alternative [approaches/headlines/intros] in different tones."
5. "Critique this. What's weak, what's confusing, what would a skeptic push back on?"

Why this matters: people overcomplicate prompts. The 80% of value comes from these five patterns applied to your actual work.

Lesson 3.4 — Voice Lock

To make AI sound like you, give it three to five samples of your own writing and tell it what's distinctive: short sentences, specific words, the rhythm. Encode that in a Skill (Claude) or a Custom GPT (ChatGPT). From then on, when you ask for a draft, it comes back recognizable.

The voice-lock prompt: "Here are three pieces I've written. Identify what's distinctive about my voice — sentence length, vocabulary, rhythm, the things I do that AI doesn't. Then write a 150-word version of [topic] in that voice."

By end of day three: AI sounds like you, not like AI. Your reusable assistants have your voice baked in.

Day 04

The Reusable Playbook system.

This is the unlock. The moment you stop using AI like a one-off tool and start treating it like a system. Every other day builds on this one.

Lesson 4.1 — Turn a Chat Where You Nailed It Into a Reusable Playbook

The problem: you spend an hour iterating with AI on a task, finally get it right, close the chat, and the next time the same task comes up, you start from scratch. Everything you taught it disappears. The fix is three steps.

Step 1: Find a chat where you nailed it. A recurring task. A back-and-forth that ended in exactly the result you wanted. Stay in that same chat — don't open a new one. Step 2: Ask AI to extract the playbook. Use the prompt below. Step 3: Test it in a new chat. Open a fresh chat, paste the playbook, run the same task. If the result holds, you have a process. Better — wrap it in a Custom GPT, Gem, or Claude Skill.

The Playbook Extraction Prompt
Based on everything we did in this conversation, extract the full process that led to this final result and turn it into a detailed reusable playbook for this task.

I want a structured playbook that captures how we moved from the initial idea or draft to the final result, including the key steps, adjustments, and decisions that improved the output along the way.

Please include:
- The type of task this playbook is for
- The starting point (what we began with)
- The final result we were aiming for
- The step-by-step process we followed in the correct order
- The key instructions, prompts, refinements, and adjustments made along the way
- The tone, structure, format, or style decisions that improved the result
- What did not work or what we changed during the process
- What should stay consistent each time
- What can be customized for each new version
- A final reusable version of the playbook written clearly enough that I can paste it into a new chat or use it to build a custom AI assistant for this recurring task

Important:
- Do not summarize the conversation
- Focus on the process that led to the final result
- Keep the playbook clear, structured, and detailed
- Make it practical and reusable for recurring tasks

Mistake to avoid: treating the playbook as written in stone. Refine it. Your standards evolve. The playbook should too.

Lesson 4.2 — From Playbook to Shareable SOP

Once you have a playbook for yourself, the next move is making it run for someone else. Take the playbook, paste it into a fresh chat, and ask: "Rewrite this as a Standard Operating Procedure that someone with no context could follow. Add a 'Why this step matters' note for each step." You now have an SOP your team or a contractor can run.

Lesson 4.3 — The Handoff Prompt

When a chat goes off the rails — context too long, model getting confused, you need to switch tools — don't start from scratch. Hand off. Ask AI to summarize the conversation as a handoff prompt: the goal, what's been decided, what's still open, the constraints, the next step. Paste it into a fresh chat. Continue from there.

The Handoff Prompt
Summarize this entire conversation as a handoff prompt I can paste into a fresh chat to continue from exactly where we are now.

Include:
- The goal we are working toward
- What we have decided so far and why
- What is still open
- The constraints (audience, format, tone, deadlines)
- The next concrete step

Write it as a single block I can copy.

Lesson 4.4 — Let AI Write Your Prompt

Stuck on how to ask? Describe the task in plain English and ask AI to write the prompt for you. "I want to do X. Write me the best prompt to give to ChatGPT to accomplish this. Include all five blocks: instruction, context, format, tone, clarifying questions." Most of the prompts you'll save and reuse forever started as AI-written.

By end of day four: three of your weekly tasks live as reusable playbooks. Hours saved every week, compounding from here.

Day 05

Content that sounds like you.

Marketing-grade content without the marketing-grade time sink. By the end of today, you have a content engine, not a content panic.

Lesson 5.1 — From Rough Idea to Publish-Ready LinkedIn Post

The pipeline has five stages: idea (a half-thought you'd normally lose), angle (the specific lens that makes it not generic), draft (in your voice, via your Custom GPT or Skill), polish (cut every sentence that doesn't earn its place), ship (with two alternate hooks for next time).

The voice piece is the hardest. Without it, every post sounds like every other LinkedIn post. With it, the post sounds like a thing only you would say. Lock voice with a Skill or GPT on day 3 — then today is mostly idea capture and angle.

The post pipeline prompt
Here's a rough idea: [your half-thought].

Take it through five stages:
1. Sharpen the angle — what's the specific lens that makes this not generic?
2. Draft a 150-word LinkedIn post in my voice (refer to the voice samples).
3. Self-critique: what's weak, what's filler, what would a skeptic push back on?
4. Polished v2 incorporating the critique.
5. Two alternate hooks I can A/B with.

Lesson 5.2 — From Blank Page to 7-Day Content Plan

Sundays should not be content panic. Run one prompt that produces a Day × Topic × Hook × Angle × CTA table for the week. The variables you supply: your audience, your offers, your monthly theme. AI handles the variation across the week so each post hits a different angle.

7-day content plan prompt
Create a 7-day content calendar for [platform] for an audience of [audience].

My monthly theme: [theme].
My current offer: [offer or CTA goal].

Format as a table with columns: Day, Topic, Hook, Post angle, CTA.
Make sure the seven days vary in angle (story, framework, contrarian take, behind-the-scenes, lesson, list, question) so it doesn't feel repetitive.

If you need clarification on my voice or offer details before drafting, ask first.

Lesson 5.3 — From Random Headlines to Clear Positioning

Most LinkedIn feeds read as a stream of "thoughts." That doesn't position you. Positioning means people can finish the sentence "she's the person who ___." The exercise: list your last 30 posts, ask AI to identify the implicit positioning and the implicit audience. Most of the time, the answer is "your positioning is unclear." Then ask AI to draft three crisper positions and three weeks of content for each. Pick one. Commit.

Lesson 5.4 — Newsletters That People Actually Open

The newsletter playbook is similar to the post pipeline but with structure. Every newsletter has the same sections (intro hook, main idea, one tactical thing, one thing to read/watch, sign-off). AI handles the variation inside the structure. Lock the structure in a Custom GPT, run it weekly with new ideas as input.

Lesson 5.5 — Ads with ChatGPT + Canva

Concept in ChatGPT (audience, offer, three angles, three hooks per angle, two visual concepts each), then Canva for the actual creative. The pattern: AI for concept and copy, Canva for execution. Stop trying to make AI tools do design. Stop trying to make design tools do strategy.

By end of day five: you have a content engine. Mondays are about ideas, not panic.

Day 06

Visuals: from concept to publish.

You stop apologizing for stock photos. You stop hiring out for things that take 10 minutes now.

Lesson 6.1 — Free, Fast Image Gen with Nano Banana (Gemini)

Gemini's image model is free and surprisingly good, especially for edits. The killer feature is consistency: you can generate a character, then ask for "the same character in a different setting" and actually get the same character. Use it for hero images, blog illustrations, social tiles, on-brand mockups.

The prompt formula: subject + style + setting + mood + technical (lighting, composition, aspect ratio). Be specific. "A woman" gets you anyone. "A 40-year-old Filipino-American woman in a navy blazer, mid-laugh, golden hour kitchen, shallow depth of field, 16:9" gets you what you wanted.

Lesson 6.2 — Professional Headshot for Free

Upload a clear photo of your face, ask Gemini for "a professional LinkedIn-style headshot" with specifics: lighting, background, attire. Iterate. Three rounds of edits gets you to publishable. The trick: include "preserve facial features exactly" in the prompt or you'll get a different person back.

Lesson 6.3 — Restyle Product Images

For product shots that need to look premium without a photographer: upload the existing image, ask for a specific premium aesthetic ("editorial fashion catalog, soft natural light, marble surface, minimal styling"), keep the product unchanged. This is the reason small-business product photos no longer have to look like Etsy listings.

Lesson 6.4 — Stories and Reels in Canva AI

Templates as scaffolds. You don't design from scratch — you customize a template that's already 80% there. Canva's AI does the heavy lifting on copy, layout, and on-brand color/font matching. Best for high-volume social where you'd otherwise burn hours.

Lesson 6.5 — On-Brand Asset Generation with Pomelli

Pomelli is Google Labs' brand-aware generator. You feed it your brand kit (colors, fonts, logo, voice), it generates assets that respect those rules. Best for marketing teams who need consistency at volume. Different from Canva: Canva is template-driven, Pomelli is brand-driven.

By end of day six: every post you publish has a visual you actually like, made in minutes, not hours.

Day 07

Video and audio without a studio.

The studio fits in a tab now. The bar for shipping audio and video has dropped through the floor.

Lesson 7.1 — Script to Short-Form Video with Pictory

Pictory takes a script and turns it into a branded short-form video with stock B-roll, captions, and voiceover. No camera, no editing software. The workflow: write the script in ChatGPT, paste into Pictory, pick a brand kit, ship. Best for content where your face isn't the point.

Lesson 7.2 — Voiceovers with ElevenLabs (v3)

ElevenLabs v3 has true emotional inflection. Not "robot reading text" but "actor reading text." You can clone your own voice in five minutes (record a few minutes of clean audio, upload). Then any text you write becomes you reading it — in any of 30+ languages.

Practical use: voiceovers for short videos, podcast intros when you don't want to re-record, multilingual versions of your existing content. The ethical line: be transparent that it's a clone if you're using it externally.

Lesson 7.3 — AI Avatar with HeyGen + ElevenLabs

HeyGen creates a video avatar of you (or of a stock person) speaking your script. Pair with your ElevenLabs voice clone and you have a scalable spokesperson — same voice, same face, infinite content. Best for course videos, FAQ videos, social explainers where you'd otherwise have to record.

Lesson 7.4 — Stop Re-Recording. Get AI Feedback.

The trap: you record a take, hate it, re-record, hate that one too, three hours gone. Instead: record once, get a transcript, paste it to AI with "give me feedback on clarity, pacing, hooks, and structure. Tell me what to cut and what to add. Then suggest three opening lines that would make me click." Re-record from the feedback. Or, often, don't re-record at all and just edit.

Lesson 7.5 — The Current Tool Landscape

Top of mind right now: Veo 3 (Google) and Adobe Firefly for video generation; HeyGen for avatars; Pictory for B-roll style; ElevenLabs for voice; Adobe Podcast and Suno for audio cleanup and music. Don't try to learn all of them. Pick the one that matches the kind of content you ship most. Master that one.

By end of day seven: you ship video and audio without rebooking your week.

Day 08

Research like a pro.

You stop wasting hours on Google. You start triaging more information in less time, with better signal.

Lesson 8.1 — Perplexity: Sourced Search

Perplexity is what you reach for when you need facts with citations. Every claim is sourced. Use it when you'd otherwise be checking three Wikipedia tabs and an industry report. Pro mode picks the right model and search depth for the question. Pages turn a research session into a shareable write-up.

The when-to-use: factual questions, market research, competitive analysis, anything where the source matters.

Lesson 8.2 — ChatGPT Deep Research

Different beast. Deep Research takes 5 to 30 minutes, browses dozens of sources, returns a structured report with citations. Use for market sizing, big-decision research, anything you'd previously have hired a consultant for. The cost is time, not money. Don't run it for quick lookups.

The framing matters. A vague research question gets a vague report. A specific question — "What are the three most successful coaching businesses serving Filipino-American women founders, and what's their pricing/positioning?" — gets a report you can use.

Lesson 8.3 — NotebookLM: Pin a Corpus, Query Anything

NotebookLM is the move when you have a body of content (PDFs, transcripts, web pages, your own notes) and want to ask questions against it. Upload up to 50 sources. Ask. Every answer cites the source. It does not hallucinate outside your corpus.

The killer feature is the audio overview — it generates a 10-minute "podcast" between two AI hosts discussing your sources. Listen on a walk. You retain more than from reading. Useful for long industry reports, your own back-catalog of writing, customer interview transcripts, books you don't have time to read cover to cover.

Lesson 8.4 — Daily Brief via ChatGPT Tasks

Tasks lets you schedule prompts to run on a schedule. The classic setup: every weekday at 7am, run "Search for the top 3 stories in [your industry] from the last 24 hours. For each, give me a 50-word summary, why it matters to me, and what I might do about it. Format as a single email-ready brief." It runs while you sleep. You wake up to your brief.

By end of day eight: you triage 10x more information in less time, with better signal.

Day 09

Sales and outbound that doesn't feel gross.

Cold messages that earn replies. Conversations that turn into clients. Without the sleaze.

Lesson 9.1 — Cold Messages That Land

The structure: Open (specific, not flattering, show you actually looked), Context (why now, why them, why you), Ask (one specific, low-friction yes). The mistake people make is leading with themselves. The fix is leading with what you noticed about the recipient.

Cold message prompt
Write three cold message variants to [name/role] at [company].

What I noticed about them: [specific thing — recent post, change at company, mutual connection, work I admire].
What I do: [one-line offer].
The ask: [low-friction next step — 15-min call, opinion on X, intro to Y].

Variants:
1. Warm and human (story-led)
2. Direct and brief (under 50 words)
3. Provocative (lead with a contrarian observation)

Tone: confident, not desperate. Specific, not generic. Short.

Lesson 9.2 — The Reply Library

The time-saver that compounds. List every recurring message you write — intros, follow-ups, scheduling, no-thank-yous, refund replies, hand-offs. For each, draft three versions: warm, neutral, firm. Save them in a Project (ChatGPT) or as a Skill (Claude). Tag by situation.

When a real message comes in, paste it in and ask AI to pick the right template and personalize. Replies stop feeling like ten decisions and start feeling like one.

Lesson 9.3 — Find Real Gaps in the Market

Stop guessing what to offer. Run customer interview transcripts (or your own discovery call transcripts) through NotebookLM. Ask: "What problems come up repeatedly that no one is solving well? What language do people use to describe those problems? What have they already tried that didn't work?" The answers write your next offer.

Lesson 9.4 — Inbox Safety Filter

Paste any suspicious email into AI with: "Is this a scam? Walk me through the signals — the red flags, the social engineering patterns, what they want me to do, and what I should do instead." Five seconds, peace of mind.

By end of day nine: outbound is faster, sharper, more honest. Ironically, AI helps you sound more like yourself, not less.

Day 10

Slides, docs, and deliverables.

The boring stuff that ate your week. Handed off.

Lesson 10.1 — PowerPoint Faster with ChatGPT

The pipeline: outline in ChatGPT (audience, goal, key sections, story arc), have it generate slide-by-slide content with speaker notes, export as PPTX, polish in PowerPoint. The trick is asking for "slide-by-slide markdown with title, bullets, and speaker notes per slide." That structure converts cleanly to slides.

Lesson 10.2 — Gamma AI: Generative Decks

Different approach. Gamma takes a topic or outline and generates a full deck with modern aesthetics. Best for decks where design matters and you don't have a designer. Edit in-place. Export to PDF or PPTX. Best for pitch decks, internal proposals, thought leadership.

Lesson 10.3 — Landing Page in Replit (No Code)

"Build me a landing page for [offer] with sections for [hero, value props, testimonials, CTA]. Use [color palette]. Mobile-responsive." Replit builds it. You iterate by chatting. Deploy to a live URL in minutes. The reason this is in the curriculum: a working landing page in 30 minutes beats a perfect plan for a designer in two weeks.

Lesson 10.4 — Boring Doc to Web Page People Read

The ask: "Take this doc and turn it into a single-page website that's actually pleasant to read. Use clear sections, callouts for key points, real visual hierarchy. Output as HTML I can deploy." Now your one-page strategy doc is a URL you can share, and people will actually scroll it.

Lesson 10.5 — Universal Organizer Prompt

For when you have a pile of stuff and don't know what to do with it: "Here's a dump of [notes/links/ideas/tasks]. Cluster them into themes. For each theme, identify the highest-leverage action. Format as a one-page focus map." Works on Sunday-night brain dumps, on quarterly reviews, on the loose ends after a busy week.

By end of day ten: deliverables that used to take a day take an hour.

Day 11

Operate like a system.

From doing AI to running on AI. Automation that compounds.

Lesson 11.1 — Claude Cowork: Folder-Based Workflows

This is the new operator pattern. Cowork talks to a folder on your computer. Drop in a folder of receipts, it builds the spreadsheet. Drop in a folder of meeting notes, it produces a summary doc. The receipts-to-spreadsheet workflow is the canonical example, but the pattern generalizes.

Why it matters: AI moves from "thing I chat with" to "thing that operates on my files." You stop copy-pasting. The work happens where your work already lives.

Lesson 11.2 — Claude AI vs. Cowork vs. Code

Claude AI (the chat product): for thinking, writing, analysis. Claude Cowork: for operating on local files and folders without code. Claude Code: for engineering work in a terminal or IDE. Pick the right surface for the job. Most people only need AI and Cowork.

Lesson 11.3 — A Team of Specialized Assistants

One Project per role. "Editor" Project that critiques drafts. "Researcher" Project loaded with industry sources. "Strategist" Project loaded with your business context. Each has its own custom instructions, its own files, its own personality. You move work between them like you'd move it between team members.

Lesson 11.4 — The Current LLM Leaderboard

State of play in 2026: Claude wins on long-form writing and careful reasoning. ChatGPT wins on ecosystem (Projects, GPTs, Memory, Tasks, Canvas, Code Interpreter). Gemini wins on Workspace integration and free image gen. Perplexity wins on sourced research. They're all good now. Pick based on workflow fit, not benchmarks.

By end of day eleven: you have at least one workflow that runs without you typing.

Day 12

Think clearer. Lead clearer.

The non-obvious uses. AI as a thinking partner, not just a doer.

Lesson 12.1 — Associative Thinking with AI

The exercise: "Take this idea: [X]. Force five lateral connections to fields you'd never expect — biology, music, urban planning, sports, ancient history. For each, identify the move from that field that I could steal." Generates novelty under pressure. Best for stuck on positioning, stuck on naming, stuck on a structural problem.

Lesson 12.2 — The Think-It-Through Framework

For decisions: "I'm trying to decide between A and B. Steelman both. Then play devil's advocate against my likely choice. Then identify the second-order effects I'm not seeing. Then summarize what a wise advisor would tell me." The point isn't that AI decides. The point is that AI surfaces the considerations you'd otherwise skip.

Lesson 12.3 — AI as Memory Investigator

"There's a thing — I think it was a book or framework — that talks about [vague description]. Help me figure out what it was." For the moments your brain blanks. Surprisingly effective for finding half-remembered references.

Lesson 12.4 — The 10-Minute Brain Dump

Open a chat. Type for 10 minutes — every loose thread, every worry, every half-idea. Then: "Cluster everything I just said. Identify the three biggest themes. For each, give me the highest-leverage next action and the one thing I'm avoiding." Walk away with a one-page focus map. Use Mondays.

Lesson 12.5 — Capture Without Losing

Voice notes anywhere (phone, walk, drive). Pipe them into AI weekly with: "Here are my voice notes from this week. Cluster them. Identify recurring themes. Surface anything that sounds like a decision I made or an idea I want to build on. Format as: themes, actions, ideas-to-revisit." Capture without losing.

By end of day twelve: AI is part of how you think, not just how you produce.

Day 13

Mindset for the long game.

The expert series. The framing that holds the whole stack together. These aren't tutorials. They're the disposition shifts that keep you sane while everything moves fast.

Lesson 13.1 — Marie Forleo: Clear the Noise, Free Your Genius

The session is less about AI than about the conditions that make AI useful. Most people don't lack tools. They lack space. The premise: your best work needs uncluttered attention, and AI's promise is only redeemed when you reclaim attention rather than fill it with new busy.

Decide what gets your attention before AI gets to choose. Use AI to remove noise (inbox triage, summarization, prep), not to add it (endless brainstorm sessions that make you feel productive but produce nothing). The 80/20 of personal productivity is still mindset. No prompt fixes a calendar with no protected time.

Lesson 13.2 — Zack Kass: Optimism as a Strategy

Former OpenAI head of go-to-market. The argument: pessimism feels smart, but it's a bad operating posture for a window of historic upside. Optimism is not naïve. It's a strategy.

Bet on abundance: assume the technology will keep getting better and cheaper, and plan accordingly. Identify your "irreplaceable layer" — the judgment, taste, and relationships that AI amplifies but doesn't substitute for. Optimism is a posture you choose, and it changes what you build, not just how you feel.

Lesson 13.3 — Amjad Masad: From Idea to Impact Without Code

Replit's founder. The thesis: the bottleneck on building has collapsed. The new constraint is taste — knowing what's worth building.

Build the thing first. Don't pitch, don't plan, don't pre-design. Build a working version, then iterate. Treat ideas as software: versioned, deployed, replaced, not protected. The new ladder is "make → ship → learn." AI compresses the loop from weeks to hours.

Lesson 13.4 — Sabrina Ramonov: Stop Chasing Tools

The most operationally useful session in the series. The frame: tools are a trap if they precede problems.

Start with the recurring task, not the tool. List every weekly task. Score by time-cost and frustration. Solve the top three. Map the human steps before the AI ones. If you can't describe what a person would do, AI can't do it either. Then wrap the workflow in a Custom GPT, Gem, Skill, or Project. Until it's wrapped, it's not a system.

Lesson 13.5 — Map Your Irreplaceable Strengths

The exercise: list your top 10 wins of the past year. Have AI cluster them. For each cluster, ask: what specific judgment, taste, or relationship made this happen? Those are your irreplaceable strengths. Build everything else around them. Delegate the rest, to people, or to AI.

By end of day thirteen: you have a relationship with AI that doesn't depend on the next model release.

Day 14

Run your week.

Make it stick. Build the cadences that turn AI from a project into a habit.

Lesson 14.1 — One-Habit New Year System

Most resolutions fail because they're too big. The fix: pick one habit. Make it small enough to do on your worst day. Use AI to design the trigger, the action, and the reward. "I want to build [habit]. Help me design the smallest possible version that I could do in under 5 minutes on my worst day. Then design a trigger that will reliably remind me, and a reward that will reinforce it."

Lesson 14.2 — Track Key Ideas in Long Conversations

Long ChatGPT chats produce great ideas that get buried. The fix: at the end of every long session, ask "List every decision we made and every idea worth saving. Format as a clean memo with sections: Decisions, Ideas to Build On, Open Questions." Save the memo somewhere persistent. Now nothing important gets lost in scroll.

Lesson 14.3 — From Vague Intent to Clear Starting Point in 10 Minutes

For when you know AI could help with X but you don't know how to start: "I want to use AI to help with [vague task]. Walk me through what would need to be true for AI to do this well, what context I'd need to provide, what tools fit best, and what the first 10 minutes of work would look like. Then ask me one clarifying question." Stops the procrastination spiral.

Lesson 14.4 — The Weekly Cadence

The rhythm that holds: Monday — 10-minute brain dump, focus map for the week. Mid-week — try one new thing (a hack, a tool, a prompt). Friday — review what worked, what to put in the playbook library, what to delete. Sunday — content plan for the week, no panic. Build it as repeating calendar events. Don't trust yourself to remember.

Lesson 14.5 — Track Time Saved

The thing nobody does, that proves the whole stack: at the end of every week, log "what AI saved me this week" — three things, with rough hours. After a month, you have evidence. After three months, you have an unanswerable case for keeping the habit. After a year, you can point to weeks of life back.

By end of day fourteen: AI is a habit, not a project. You don't think about it. You just operate this way now.

The 5-Block Prompt Template

The single most reused thing in the club. Works in ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, anywhere.

Instruction: [What you want the AI to do.]
Context: [Relevant background information.]
Format: [How the output should be structured.]
Tone: [Communication style if relevant.]
Clarifying Questions: If you need more information or clarification to do this well and help me reach my goal, ask me the questions you need before you answer.

Why it works: a vague prompt gets a vague answer. The five blocks force you to be specific about what, why, how it should look, how it should sound, and gives the AI permission to ask before it guesses.

Worked example — handling a price increase:

Instruction: Draft an email response to a client who is unhappy about a price increase for my consulting services.
Context: I am a solo consultant and raised my rates by 20% starting next month. This client has been with me for two years at the old rate. I want to keep them as a client but cannot continue offering the old price. As a gesture of goodwill, I'm offering them the previous rate for one more month.
Format: Professional email with a clear subject line.
Tone: Warm and appreciative, but firm about the value of the services.
Clarifying Questions: Ask me if you need details about the specific services or the new pricing structure before writing the email.

The Reusable Playbook (3 steps)

Stop losing the chat where you nailed it. Turn it into a process you can run again in seconds.

  1. Find a chat where you nailed it.

    A task you do regularly. A back-and-forth that ended in exactly the result you wanted. Stay in that same chat.

  2. Ask AI to extract the playbook.

    Paste this prompt:

    Based on everything we did in this conversation, extract the full process that led to this final result and turn it into a detailed reusable playbook for this task.

I want a structured playbook that captures how we moved from the initial idea or draft to the final result, including the key steps, adjustments, and decisions that improved the output along the way.

Please include:
- The type of task this playbook is for
- The starting point (what we began with)
- The final result we were aiming for
- The step-by-step process we followed in the correct order
- The key instructions, prompts, refinements, and adjustments made along the way
- The tone, structure, format, or style decisions that improved the result
- What did not work or what we changed during the process
- What should stay consistent each time
- What can be customized for each new version
- A final reusable version of the playbook written clearly enough that I can paste it into a new chat or use it to build a custom AI assistant for this recurring task

Important:
- Do not summarize the conversation
- Focus on the process that led to the final result
- Keep the playbook clear, structured, and detailed
- Make it practical and reusable for recurring tasks

  3. Test it in a new chat.

    Open a fresh chat. Paste the playbook (or upload it as a doc). Run the same task. If the result holds, you have a process. Save it. Better — turn it into a Custom GPT, a Gem, or a Claude Skill.

Mistake to avoid: treating the playbook as written in stone. Refine it. Your standards evolve. The playbook should too.

The Problem-First AI System

From Sabrina Ramonov's expert session. The framework for not losing your mind every time a new tool drops.

1

Name the recurring problem.

"I waste an hour every Monday writing the same client update." Specific. Recurring. Painful.

2

Define the win.

What does "solved" look like? Time saved? Quality lifted? Consistency?

3

Map the steps a human would take.

Not the AI. A human. This is the playbook draft.

4

Pick the tool that fits the steps.

ChatGPT for chat-style ops. Claude for long-form, careful writing. Gemini for things that hook into Google Workspace. Replit for when "build me a web page" is faster than "find me a tool."

5

Wrap it in a reusable shell.

Custom GPT, Gem, Skill, or Project. Stop typing setup every time.

The Reply Library

Stop rewriting the same message. Build a vault.

  1. List every message you find yourself rewriting (intro DMs, follow-ups, no-thank-yous, hand-offs, refund replies, scheduling).
  2. For each, draft three versions: warm, neutral, firm.
  3. Save them in a Project (ChatGPT) or as a Skill (Claude). Tag by situation.
  4. When a real message comes in, paste it in and ask AI to pick the right template and personalize it.

The point: mental energy is the bottleneck, not typing speed. Replies should feel like one decision, not ten.

The Handoff Prompt

When a chat goes off the rails, don't start over. Hand off.

Summarize this entire conversation as a handoff prompt I can paste into a fresh chat to continue from exactly where we are now.

Include:
- The goal we are working toward
- What we have decided so far and why
- What is still open
- The constraints (audience, format, tone, deadlines)
- The next concrete step

Write it as a single block I can copy.
